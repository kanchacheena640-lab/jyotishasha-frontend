# Jyotishasha Authority Platform — Content Rules

**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** Production — Frozen  
**Applies to:** All authority pages across all domains  
**Scope:** AI-generated content, human-edited content, and all hybrid workflows  

This document is the editorial contract for the Jyotishasha Authority Platform. Every authority page — regardless of domain, topic, or generation method — is held to these standards. When in doubt, resolve by reference to Section 1 (Editorial Philosophy).

---

## 1. Editorial Philosophy

These principles are not style guidelines. They are non-negotiable. Every section on every page is evaluated against them.

**1.1 Practical Before Theoretical**  
Every page exists to help the reader understand or decide something. Present the practical implication before the astrological theory. A reader asking "when will I get a job?" needs to understand what to look for in their chart before they need to know why those factors matter. Theory that serves no practical understanding does not belong on the page.

**1.2 Explain Before Predicting**  
State the astrological mechanism before the outcome. Never present a conclusion without showing the reasoning. "Jupiter transiting the 10th house draws career attention — and when this aligns with an active 10th-lord dasha, job opportunities often materialise" is authoritative. "Jupiter in the 10th means a new job" is not.

**1.3 Never Claim Certainty**  
Astrology is a system of indicators, not guarantees. No page should state that a specific outcome will happen. Language communicates probability and pattern, not inevitability. "This combination frequently coincides with career transitions" is accurate. "This guarantees a promotion" is not. The distinction is not a disclaimer — it is what the system actually says.

**1.4 Avoid Fear-Based Writing**  
Do not frame planetary positions as inherently dangerous, cursed, or destined to destroy. Saturn's dasha is not "bad." Rahu is not "malevolent." Ketu does not "bring ruin." Every planetary combination has a range of expression depending on chart specifics. Fear-based language misrepresents Jyotisha and erodes the trust that authority content depends on.

**1.5 Respect Genuine Uncertainty**  
Some questions cannot be answered from a chart alone. Some topics have real interpretive disagreement among experienced practitioners. Acknowledging this is a mark of authority, not weakness. "Classical texts differ on this point" and "In practice, this varies significantly by ascendant" are stronger statements than false certainty.

**1.6 Every Page Helps the Reader Decide**  
The purpose of each page is to improve the reader's ability to understand their situation, evaluate their timing, or make a better decision. A page that does not contribute to this purpose — that exists to fill space, add a URL, or insert keywords — fails the platform's mission regardless of how technically correct it is.

**1.7 Write for the Thoughtful Skeptic**  
The primary reader is someone who may not fully believe in astrology but is open to using it as a framework for reflection and timing. Do not write for the uncritical believer who accepts every planetary claim at face value. Do not write for the dismissive skeptic either. Write for the thoughtful person who wants to understand what the system says and why — and then decide for themselves.

**1.8 Specificity Over Generality**  
A statement about "Jupiter in the 10th house during Saturn's dasha for a Libra ascendant" is more authoritative than "Jupiter is the planet of expansion and career growth." Specificity signals expertise. Generality signals content farming. Every section must contain at least one insight specific enough that it could not appear unchanged on a generic astrology site.

## 2. Voice & Tone

**2.1 Professional Without Being Academic**  
Write like an experienced Jyotisha practitioner speaking to a client — not like a research paper, and not like a casual blog. Complete sentences. Precise language. No jargon without immediate explanation. No over-explanation of things an engaged reader can look up.

**2.2 Trustworthy Through Specificity**  
Trust is built through specific, verifiable claims. "The 10th lord in the 6th house during Saturn's dasha often correlates with employment through competitive processes, institutions, or service roles" is trustworthy. "Saturn brings discipline and hard work to your career path" is not. If a sentence could appear unchanged in any generic article about Saturn, it does not belong on this page.

**2.3 Approachable Through Clarity**  
Define every Sanskrit or technical term the first time it appears in each section. After defining it, use the term without re-defining it. Do not assume the reader knows what a "yoga," "bhava," "antardasha," or "atmakaraka" is unless the page's `authorityLevel` is `'comprehensive'`.

**2.4 Consistent Terminology**  
On every page, choose one name for each concept and use it throughout. Do not alternate between "10th house," "10th bhava," and "career house" on the same page. Standard usage for this platform:

| Concept | Standard Term | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Ascendant | "ascendant" or "lagna" (define on first use) | "rising sign" |
| House | "house" (e.g. "10th house") | "bhava" alone without prior definition |
| Dasha period | "dasha" with planet name (e.g. "Saturn's dasha") | "planetary period" as primary term |
| Sub-period | "antardasha" (define on first use) | "sub-period" or "sub-dasha" |
| Transit | "transit" | "gochara" without definition |
| Natal chart | "birth chart" or "natal chart" | "horoscope" for the chart document |
| Malefic | "functional malefic" or "natural malefic" (be specific) | "bad planet" |
| Benefic | "functional benefic" or "natural benefic" (be specific) | "good planet" |

**2.5 No Sensational Headlines**  
Headings are descriptive labels, not dramatic reveals. "Planets That Support a New Job" is a heading. "These 3 Planets Will Change Your Career Forever" is not. A section heading accurately describes what the section contains — nothing more, nothing implied.

**2.6 No Clickbait**  
No content designed to generate clicks through surprise, anxiety, or manufactured urgency. This includes: "What most astrologers won't tell you," "The secret planet behind career failure," "Why your love life is astrologically blocked," "The truth about Saturn that no one shares." These patterns destroy the authority that this platform is built to establish.

**2.7 Sanskrit Terms: Use Precisely, Define Always**  
Sanskrit terms are used only when they name a concept with no precise English equivalent (e.g., "yogakaraka," "atmakaraka," "vargottama," "neecha bhanga"). When used, define the term in the same sentence or immediately after. Do not use Sanskrit to sound authoritative. Use it when it adds technical precision that the English term cannot provide.

Acceptable: "A yogakaraka — a planet that rules both a Kendra (angular) and a Trikona (trine) house in the same chart — carries exceptional potency for that ascendant."  
Unacceptable: "The yogakaraka creates raj yoga and elevates the bhava sthana's significations."

**2.8 Prohibited Phrases**  
The following are clichés that weaken authority or misrepresent Jyotisha. They must not appear on any page:

- "Astrology has been practiced for thousands of years"
- "The stars have aligned"
- "The universe is sending you a message"
- "According to ancient wisdom"
- "This is written in your stars"
- "Your destiny is revealed in your chart"
- "Everything happens for a reason"
- "Trust the cosmic plan"
- "Unlock the secrets of your chart"
- "What the planets are telling you"
- "Revealed: the truth about [planet]"

## 3. Page Structure

The required structure for all authority topic pages. **[Required]** items must appear on every page. **[Conditional]** items appear when the topic warrants them.

```
1. Hero                        [Required]
2. Main Content Sections       [Required — 2 to 6 sections]
3. CTA                         [Required — 1 to 2]
4. FAQ                         [Required — 4 to 8 questions]
5. Disclaimer                  [Conditional]
```

Related topics and breadcrumb navigation are rendered automatically by the engine from topic metadata. They are not authored as content sections.

**3.1 Hero**  
Sets context and engages the reader. The `hero.headline` is the H1. The `hero.subtext` is the opening paragraph directly beneath it. Together they must: name the topic, frame the primaryQuestion, and establish why the reader should continue. The hero does not fully answer the question — it demonstrates that the page will.

**3.2 Main Content Sections**  
2 to 6 sections. Each uses the most appropriate layout for its content type. Sections are ordered from most to least important: a reader who stops after one section has received maximum value. Section titles describe what the reader will learn, not what the section contains structurally.

**3.3 CTA**  
Placed after all main content sections, before the FAQ. Never interrupts a content section. Always relevant to the page's topic. See Section 9 for full CTA rules.

**3.4 FAQ**  
4 to 8 questions. Always the final substantive section. FAQ covers questions not answered in the main sections — it extends understanding, it does not repeat it. See Section 7 for full FAQ rules.

**3.5 Disclaimer**  
Required on pages that: discuss timing for major life events with any specificity, address health or medical matters, or provide directional guidance on significant decisions. The disclaimer is brief (2–3 sentences), placed at the end of the page, and clarifies the educational and reflective nature of the content. It is not an apology — it is a calibration of appropriate reader expectation.

## 4. Heading Rules

**4.1 H1 (`hero.headline`)**  
- Exactly one per page. Never more.
- Written for engagement — not for keyword density.
- Length: 40–90 characters.
- May be a question or a declarative statement. Never a sentence fragment.
- Must name the topic clearly. A reader seeing only the H1 must understand exactly what the page covers.
- The focus keyword should appear in the H1 naturally — not forced to the front at the expense of readability.

**4.2 H2 (`PageSection.title`)**  
- Used for major section titles only.
- Descriptive noun phrases — not questions (questions belong in FAQ), not commands, not clever wordplay.
- Length: 30–65 characters.
- Must work as standalone navigation labels. A reader scanning only the H2s must understand the page's structure and scope.
- The focus keyword should appear in at least one H2, in the section where it is most naturally relevant.
- No two H2s on the same page begin with the same word.
- H2s set expectations the section must meet. Do not write a heading more specific or more general than the section's content.

**4.3 H3 (within-section sub-headings)**  
- Used sparingly. Only when a section contains two or more clearly distinct sub-topics that benefit from separate labels.
- H3s are not available as a `PageSection` construct. They appear only within long Accordion or List item labels.
- Length: 20–50 characters.
- Must be subordinate to the H2 above in scope.

**4.4 Heading Prohibitions**  
- No duplicate headings anywhere on the page (across all heading levels).
- H1 must not duplicate any H2.
- No headings that imply withheld information ("The Real Reason Your Saturn Dasha Is Hard").
- No headings that over-promise ("Everything You Need to Know About the 10th House").
- No headings ending in "..." or implying incompleteness.
- No clickbait framing at any heading level.

## 5. Introduction Rules

The introduction is the `hero.subtext` field. It is the most important text on the page — it determines whether the reader continues.

**5.1 Length**  
100–280 characters minimum. No upper limit for well-constructed prose. One strong paragraph. Do not split into multiple paragraphs.

**5.2 What the Introduction Must Achieve**  
In order, within a single paragraph:

1. **Reference the primaryQuestion** within the first two sentences. Not verbatim — the reader should recognise that their question is being addressed.
2. **Establish the contentAngle** — the unique editorial perspective that differentiates this page. This must be felt in the first reading, not deferred to the middle of the page.
3. **Give a directional answer** — not the full answer, but enough to confirm the reader is in the right place and will find what they came for. "The answer depends on three factors: the running dasha lord, the strength of the 10th house in the natal chart, and the current transit picture" is a directional answer. "There are many factors" is not.
4. **Create forward momentum** — the reader should want to read the next section.

**5.3 What the Introduction Must Not Do**  
- Define astrology or Jyotisha (the reader is on this site — they know what it is).
- List what the article will cover ("In this article, we will discuss...").
- Start with a dictionary definition of any term.
- Open with "In Vedic astrology," "According to Jyotisha," or "Astrology has long recognized."
- Make a claim the page cannot support in the sections that follow.
- Use the words "important" or "crucial" without immediately demonstrating why.

**5.4 The Directional Answer Principle**  
The introduction should give a reader something actionable or illuminating even if they read nothing else. This is not a complete answer — it is a signal that prevents the reader from abandoning the page before reaching the full analysis.

## 6. Section Writing Rules

**6.1 Section Length**  
- Minimum: 80 words of body content (excluding item labels in structured layouts).
- Maximum: 400 words per section.
- Recommended: 150–300 words.
- If a section consistently requires more than 350 words to make its point, it should be split into two sections or a more efficient layout should be chosen.

**6.2 Paragraph Rules**  
- 2–4 sentences per paragraph.
- 40–80 words per paragraph.
- One idea per paragraph. Do not compound two distinct analytical points into one paragraph.
- Active voice preferred. "Saturn's dasha produces results slowly" not "Results are produced slowly during Saturn's dasha."
- Lead with the main point. Do not open a paragraph with context or qualification — state the point first, then support it.

**6.3 Layout Selection**  
Choose the layout that best serves the content's natural structure. Use the first option in this list whose description fits.

| Layout | Use When | Items | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| `cards` | Items are parallel and each requires 50–120 words of description | 3–6 | Planet descriptions, house interpretations, sign profiles |
| `grid` | Items are short keywords or concepts needing minimal description | 4–12 | Nakshatra lists, remedy categories, timing windows |
| `checklist` | Items are binary conditions — either present or absent in the chart | 4–8 | Favorable indicators, caution signals, chart prerequisites |
| `timeline` | Items are sequential events or periods in chronological order | 3–8 | Dasha sequences, transit phases, life-stage progressions |
| `table` | Items compare 3 or more subjects across 2 or more consistent dimensions | 3–10 | Planet-house combinations, sign dignities, ascendant effects |
| `comparison` | Items contrast two clearly defined options or interpretations | 2–6 | Benefic vs malefic periods, natal vs transit analysis |
| `accordion` | Items each have substantial explanation (80+ words) that would overwhelm prose | 3–8 | Deep technical definitions, exception handling, layered analysis |
| `list` | Items are simple enumeration with brief labels | 3–8 | Quick tips, step sequences, brief resource references |
| `alert` | Items are cautions, important exceptions, or timing warnings | 2–5 | Retrograde cautions, combustion periods, contraindications |
| `faq` | Items answer specific reader questions | 4–8 | Common objections, clarifications, edge cases |
| `cta` | Items are calls to action linking to a tool or report | 1–2 | Tool links, report links |

**6.4 Bullets and Lists**  
- Use list or checklist layouts only when items are genuinely discrete and parallel.
- Never use bullets as a substitute for a paragraph that hasn't been written.
- Every list item must be meaningful and complete without the surrounding prose.
- Minimum 3 items in any list. If you have 2, write them as a sentence.
- Maximum 8 items in any list. If more are needed, group them into sub-categories.
- Every list section must have at least one introductory sentence before the items.

**6.5 Tables**  
- Every table requires a header row with labels 2–4 words each.
- Every cell must be filled. No empty cells.
- Tables must be readable at mobile widths. Maximum 4 columns for standard section tables.
- Do not use tables for content that flows more naturally as prose or cards.

**6.6 Section Sequence**  
Order sections from most important to least important within the topic's scope. The reader who reads only the first section should have received the most useful information. Exception: topics with a natural logical or chronological sequence (step-by-step analyses, dasha period progressions) should maintain that sequence.

**6.7 Section Independence**  
Each section must be independently readable. A reader who jumps directly to any section must be able to understand it without having read the sections before it. If a section genuinely depends on a prior section, add a single bridging sentence at the start: "The timing cautions in this section build on the planetary indicators covered above."

**6.8 Item Body Quality Standard**  
In any `cards`, `checklist`, `accordion`, or `comparison` section, when an item has a `body` field, that body must contain original analytical insight — not a restatement of the `label` in different words. If the body only repeats what the label already communicates, delete the body.

## 7. FAQ Rules

**7.1 Number of FAQs**  
- Minimum: 4 questions per page.
- Maximum: 8 questions per page.
- Recommended: 5–6 questions.

**7.2 Question Selection**  
FAQs address questions that:
- Were not directly answered in the main sections (no duplication).
- Represent real search queries for the topic — phrased as readers actually ask them.
- Address common misconceptions about the topic.
- Cover important edge cases or exceptions not suited to main sections.
- Extend understanding of the primaryQuestion without repeating what the sections already covered.

Do not write FAQ questions that:
- Are trivially answered by reading the main sections.
- Apply only to one specific, narrow chart configuration.
- Repeat each other with slight rewording.
- Require more than 120 words to answer accurately.
- Are invented by the author to fill the FAQ section rather than drawn from genuine reader questions.

**7.3 Question Style**  
- Write in first person where natural to the topic ("Is Saturn dasha always bad for job searches?").
- Phrase questions as a reader would ask them — not as an editor would categorize them.
- Each question is self-contained. A reader does not need to have read other questions to understand it.
- Do not write "What is..." questions for terms already defined in the main sections.
- Questions should be specific enough that the answer meaningfully narrows the reader's understanding.

**7.4 Answer Standards**  
- 60–120 words per answer.
- The first sentence directly answers the question. Not "That's a great question" or any acknowledgment filler. Not a restatement of the question. The answer.
- Do not end answers with a follow-up question or an invitation to consult.
- Plain prose only — no bullets, no bold text, no sub-headings within FAQ answers.
- Every answer is standalone. A reader seeing only the question and answer, without having read the page, must find it complete and useful.
- If an answer cannot be given accurately in 120 words, the question is too complex for FAQ. Consider promoting it to a main section or creating a dedicated page.

**7.5 SEO Alignment**  
- FAQ answers of 40–60 words target Google's paragraph featured snippet format.
- Questions should reflect natural language queries (how, what, which, when, why) — not formal category labels ("Explain the role of...").
- Secondary keywords from `keywords[]` should appear naturally in FAQ questions and answers.
- The FAQ section generates FAQPage JSON-LD automatically when present. Do not attempt to manually structure answers for this — write them for readers, and the schema layer handles the rest.

## 8. Internal Linking Rules

**8.1 Link Count**  
- Minimum: 2 internal links per page.
- Maximum: 4 links on standard and introductory pages. Up to 5 on comprehensive-level pages.
- Never link to the same destination more than once on a single page.

**8.2 When to Link**  
Link when a concept mentioned in the text has a dedicated page on Jyotishasha that would be genuinely useful to a reader who wants more depth on that concept. The criterion is reader utility — not keyword overlap, not link quantity, not topic adjacency.

Do not link in:
- Any heading (H1, H2, or H3).
- The introduction (`hero.subtext`).
- Alert section items (urgent content — links create distraction).
- FAQ answers (FAQ answers are self-contained by design; links disrupt this).

**8.3 Anchor Text Rules**  
- Anchor text must describe where the link goes. "Learn more" and "click here" are never acceptable.
- Use natural phrases that reflect the `linkingKeywords` of the target page (2–6 words).
- The anchor text must be meaningful to a reader scanning the page without reading the surrounding sentence.
- Do not use the target page's full title verbatim as anchor text if it sounds unnatural in context. Adapt it to the sentence.
- Do not use keyword-stuffed anchor text designed to signal to search engines rather than describe the destination.

**8.4 Link Placement**  
- Links appear in the body text of sections — not in section titles, not in the introduction, not in list item labels.
- Introduce the linked concept with enough context (1–2 sentences) before placing the link. Do not link in the first sentence of a section.
- Do not place the link in the final sentence of a section — it reads as an afterthought.
- Distribute links across different sections. Do not cluster two or more links in the same section.

**8.5 Cross-Domain Links**  
Links from one domain to another (e.g., Financial Astrology → Marriage Astrology) are appropriate only when the connection is genuinely useful to the reader on this specific page. Quantity of cross-domain links is not a goal. Quality of relevance is.

## 9. CTA Rules

**9.1 Placement and Count**  
- 1 to 2 CTAs per page. Never more.
- Placed after all main content sections, before the FAQ section.
- Never interrupt a main content section with a CTA mid-flow.
- Never place a CTA in the introduction.
- The CTA section uses `SectionLayout.CTA` and is rendered by the engine. It is not authored as inline text.

**9.2 Relevance**  
Every CTA must be directly relevant to the page's topic and primaryQuestion. A career timing page must not feature a marriage report CTA. A reader who has spent 3 minutes reading about job prospects should be offered a job-timing tool or career report — not a generic astrology product.

**9.3 Label Rules**  
- 4–8 words.
- Action-first: begins with a verb.
- Specific: names what the reader gets, not what they should do generally.
- "Check Your Job Timing" is acceptable. "Get Started" is not. "Explore the Universe of Your Chart" is not.

**9.4 Description Rules**  
- 20–50 words.
- Describes what the tool or report actually provides.
- Does not promise a specific outcome.
- Does not use urgency language ("Act now," "Limited time").
- Acceptable: "Enter your birth details to see which planets currently support a career change and the strongest months ahead."
- Not acceptable: "Discover the exact date you will land your dream job."

**9.5 Soft vs Strong CTAs**  
- **Strong CTA:** a direct call to use the tool or purchase the report. Maximum one strong CTA per page. Used when the tool or report directly answers the page's primaryQuestion.
- **Soft CTA:** a suggestion to explore a related resource. Can be secondary, following a strong CTA.
- Do not use two strong CTAs on the same page.

## 10. SEO Rules

**10.1 Title (`topic.title`)**  
- 40–60 characters including spaces.
- Keyword-first: the focus keyword or its core noun appears within the first 4 words.
- Descriptive noun phrase format — not a question, not an imperative, not a fragment.
- Must accurately describe the page. The reader who clicks this title in the SERP must find exactly what it promised.
- Does not include the domain name ("Jyotishasha") — the engine appends it at the page level.

**10.2 Meta Description (`topic.metaDescription`)**  
- 140–160 characters including spaces.
- CTA-oriented: the final clause implies or states an action.
- Contains the focus keyword naturally (not forced or repeated).
- Meaningfully different from the title: the title names the topic; the meta description explains the value of reading it.
- Unique across all pages — never reuse a meta description.
- Does not end with a period (SERP formatting preference).
- Does not quote the hero headline verbatim.

**10.3 Focus Keyword Distribution**  
The `focusKeyword` must appear in each of these locations exactly once:
- The H1 (`hero.headline`).
- Within the first 100 words of the page (`hero.subtext`).
- In at least one H2 (`PageSection.title`), in the most relevant section.
- In the meta description.

After these placements, use the focus keyword only where it arises naturally. Do not force it into every paragraph.

**10.4 Semantic Keywords**  
Supporting keywords from `keywords[]` should be distributed naturally throughout the page. A page that genuinely covers its topic will naturally use semantically related terms — they do not need to be forced. If you must consciously insert a keyword into a sentence, the sentence is probably off-topic for the section containing it.

**10.5 Keyword Prohibitions**  
- No keyword repetition within the same paragraph.
- No more than 3 uses of the focus keyword within any single section.
- No keywords in section titles unless they arise naturally from the section's descriptive label.
- No lists that are structured primarily to insert keywords rather than to provide value.

**10.6 Featured Snippet Optimization**  
- **Paragraph snippets:** Write the direct answer to the primaryQuestion in 40–60 words somewhere in the first main section. Open the answer with the topic's key noun — not "It depends" or "There are many factors to consider."
- **List snippets:** Checklist and list sections are automatically eligible. Item labels should be specific and complete — not single-word entries.
- **FAQ snippets:** FAQ answers of 40–60 words are eligible for FAQPage rich results, generated automatically from the FAQ section. Write for readers; the schema layer handles structured data.

**10.7 URL Slug**  
- 3–5 words, kebab-case.
- Matches the primary keyword phrase.
- No stop words (the, a, an, in, for, of, with).
- Acceptable: `new-job-astrology`, `saturn-dasha-career`. Not acceptable: `when-will-i-get-a-new-job-in-astrology`.

## 11. E-E-A-T Rules

**11.1 Demonstrating Experience**  
Write from an analytical, practitioner standpoint — not from a textbook summary. Reference how chart patterns present themselves in practice, including conditions, exceptions, and variations by ascendant. "A debilitated planet with a strong neecha bhanga raj yoga can outperform a nominally exalted planet afflicted by the 6th lord" demonstrates experience. "Debilitated planets are weak" does not.

**11.2 Demonstrating Expertise**  
- Reference specific technical factors: house numbers, specific planet names, named yogas, specific dasha lords.
- Distinguish between classical textbook interpretations and contemporary practitioner applications.
- When a topic is genuinely contested among experienced Jyotishis, say so explicitly and name the competing views.
- Do not make claims that cannot be traced to the classical tradition or established practitioner consensus.
- Expertise is demonstrated by knowing what the system does not say as much as by knowing what it does say.

**11.3 Demonstrating Authoritativeness**  
Authority is built through:
- **Precision:** "Jupiter's special aspects fall on the 5th, 7th, and 9th houses from its position" is authoritative. "Jupiter blesses wherever it looks" is not.
- **Conditional accuracy:** "This applies when the planet is neither combust nor in an enemy sign and is not receiving a malefic aspect from the 8th lord" demonstrates command of the full system.
- **Technical consistency:** Apply the same system of house classification, aspect rules, and dignities throughout the entire page. Do not mix Parashari and non-Parashari rules without flagging the distinction.

**11.4 Demonstrating Trustworthiness**  

Required language calibration:

| Overclaiming — Do Not Use | Calibrated — Use Instead |
|---|---|
| "will cause" | "often coincides with" |
| "guarantees" | "is one strong indicator of" |
| "always means" | "frequently indicates" |
| "Your chart says you will" | "This combination in your chart suggests" |
| "This is your destiny" | "This pattern is associated with" |
| "Impossible during this period" | "Less commonly seen during this period" |
| "X planet destroys Y house" | "X planet often challenges the themes of Y house" |
| "You must do X to avoid Y" | "Some practitioners suggest X during this period" |

**11.5 Responsible Astrology Standards**  
These standards apply to every page on the platform without exception:

1. **No specific timing certainties.** Timing windows are appropriate ("this transit window often spans 6–8 weeks"). Specific dates are not. Never state that an event will happen on a specific date or within a specific calendar month.

2. **No health diagnostics.** Pages addressing health themes discuss astrological patterns as prompts for reflection or areas of attention — they do not diagnose conditions, predict illness, or suggest medical outcomes. All pages touching health must include a disclaimer.

3. **No crisis amplification.** Do not describe any planetary period as "the worst period of your life," "a period of ruin," or imply that a reader's situation is hopeless or fated. Every chart and every period contains both challenges and resources.

4. **No displacement of professional judgment.** Where a topic touches decisions with legal, medical, financial, or relationship stakes, astrology is framed as one input into the reader's reflection — not as a replacement for professional advice. Pages addressing such topics include a disclaimer.

5. **Respect for the tradition.** Jyotisha is a living tradition with cultural, spiritual, and intellectual depth. Write about it with respect for both its practice and its practitioners — without demanding the reader's belief and without condescending to it.

## 12. AI Generation Rules

These rules apply to every AI system prompt, content generation request, and automated content workflow.

**12.1 Scope Discipline**  
Generate content only within the scope defined by `aiMetadata.topicGoal` and `aiMetadata.primaryQuestion`. Concepts listed in `aiMetadata.avoidTopics` must not appear — not as primary content, not as passing references, not as implied context. If addressing a concept would require crossing into avoidTopics territory, stop at the boundary and note the boundary explicitly.

**12.2 The Primary Question Test**  
Before finalizing any section, test it against this question: "Does this section contribute to answering the `primaryQuestion`?" If the answer is no, the section is off-topic and must be revised or removed.

**12.3 No Invented Rules**  
Do not generate planetary rules, yogas, or interpretive principles that do not exist in the classical Jyotisha tradition or in established practitioner consensus. If uncertain whether a claim is classical, omit it or explicitly frame it as a contemporary interpretation with that framing visible in the text. Invented rules passed off as classical Jyotisha directly damage E-E-A-T and the platform's authority.

**12.4 No Repetition**  
The same point must not appear in more than one section. Before finalizing a page, compare every section against every other section for duplicated arguments, duplicated examples, and duplicated language patterns. If two sections make the same point, merge them into the more precise one and remove the other.

**12.5 No Generic Filler**  
The following content patterns produce no value and must not appear in any section:

- Generic planet descriptions unrelated to the page's specific topic ("Mercury is the planet of communication, logic, and intellect...").
- Restatements of the page title or primaryQuestion in different words without adding new content.
- Transition sentences that only announce what the next section will cover.
- Closing summaries that repeat what the sections already said.
- Historical or cultural context about astrology in general that has no bearing on the specific topic.
- Padding sentences that extend word count without extending understanding.

**12.6 Content Angle Enforcement**  
The `aiMetadata.contentAngle` defines the unique perspective this page takes. It must be expressed in the introduction and maintained in every subsequent section. If a section does not reflect the content angle — if it could appear on any generic astrology site — it has not met the standard.

**12.7 Difficulty Calibration**  

| Level | Vocabulary | Technical terms | Analysis depth |
|---|---|---|---|
| `introductory` | Accessible, no assumed priors | Define every term on first use in each section | Single-factor; one concept at a time |
| `intermediate` | Standard Jyotisha vocabulary | Define uncommon or domain-specific terms | Two-factor; layered but not simultaneous |
| `comprehensive` | Full technical range | No definition required for standard terms | Multi-factor simultaneous analysis natural |

**12.8 Terminology Lock**  
Before generating content, identify the standard term for every major concept that will appear on the page (planet names, house references, key technical constructs). Lock these terms and use them exclusively throughout. Do not alternate between synonyms within the same page.

**12.9 Badge and Label Consistency**  
`SectionItem.badge` values must be 1–4 words. Badge text across items within the same section must be grammatically parallel (all nouns, all noun phrases, or all adjective-noun pairs — not mixed). Badge `badgeVariant` must accurately reflect the semantic meaning of the badge: `positive` for favorable, `negative` for challenging, `warning` for caution, `neutral` for neither, `info` for annotation.

**12.10 Hindi Content Standards**  
Hindi fields (`_hi`) are written in natural Hindi for native speakers — not machine-translated English. Standards:
- Sentence structure follows natural Hindi patterns, not English structure with Hindi words.
- Sanskrit terms may be used in Hindi content without re-definition (they are standard in Hindi-language Jyotisha discourse).
- Honorifics and register should match the English version's level of formality.
- Hindi text is reviewed by the same standards as English text — not treated as a secondary output.

**12.11 Item Body Requirement**  
When a `SectionItem` has a `body` field, the body must contain original analytical insight — not a restatement of the `label` in more words. Test: if the `body` is removed, does the reader lose something they couldn't have inferred from the `label`? If not, the body fails this standard and should be rewritten or removed.

**12.12 The Specificity Test**  
Before marking a section complete, confirm: does this section contain at least one insight specific enough that it could not appear unchanged on a generic astrology site? If every statement in the section could be found on a site that generates astrology content in bulk, the section has not met the editorial standard and must be revised.
