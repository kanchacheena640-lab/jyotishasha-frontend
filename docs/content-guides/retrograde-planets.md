## 1. Purpose of the Cluster

This cluster establishes Jyotishasha as the definitive authority on retrograde motion in Vedic astrology — both the astronomical phenomenon and its classical Jyotish interpretation.

The cluster serves two audiences simultaneously:
- Readers who want to understand what retrograde motion means in their birth chart (natal retrograde)
- Readers who want to understand what a retrograde transit means for them right now (transit retrograde)

Both audiences must be served on every page. Neither is a secondary concern.

The cluster currently covers five planets: Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn. Sun and Moon never go retrograde. Rahu and Ketu are in permanent apparent retrograde and will receive their own dedicated Planet Authority pages.

## 2. User Intent

Users arriving at these pages are in one of five states:

**Confused** — They have heard "Mercury retrograde" in popular culture and want to know if it is real and what it actually means.

**Curious** — They noticed a retrograde symbol (℞) next to a planet in their birth chart and want to understand what it means for them.

**Concerned** — They are worried about a current retrograde transit affecting their life and are looking for practical guidance.

**Research-minded** — They are students of Jyotish who want a rigorous explanation of vakri graha theory.

**Skeptical** — They are not sure whether astrology is meaningful but are open to a grounded explanation.

Every page must address the confused and concerned reader first. The research-minded reader is a secondary audience. The skeptical reader is always in the room — write for them too.

## 4. Writing Philosophy

### Primacy of specificity

Every claim must be specific enough that a reader thinks "that describes me" rather than "that could describe anyone." Vague language about retrograde being "a time of reflection" or "intense energy" is rejected at the editorial level. The writer must say what specifically happens, to whom, and why.

### Astronomy first, astrology second

Readers trust astrology more when they understand the astronomical basis. Always establish the physical phenomenon before the astrological interpretation. A reader who understands why Mercury appears to move backward is far more receptive to the Vedic interpretation than a reader who is asked to accept it on faith.

### Natal vs transit distinction

Every detail page must clearly distinguish between natal retrograde (a planet retrograde at birth, a permanent feature of the chart) and transit retrograde (a planet currently appearing retrograde, a temporary phenomenon). These are not the same. They produce different effects and require separate explanations.

### No Western contamination

The retrograde concept exists in both Western and Vedic astrology. Vedic tradition treats vakri planets differently — often as strengthened, not weakened. Do not import Western interpretations ("avoid signing contracts during Mercury retrograde") without specifically attributing them to Western astrology and distinguishing the Vedic view. When the Vedic view differs, say so clearly.

### Evergreen authority

These pages must be useful in five years, not just this month. Avoid time-specific language. Avoid referencing current events, current transits, or dates. Those belong in transit pages or blog posts.

## 5. Tone of Voice

**Authoritative.** Write as a practicing Jyotishi with deep knowledge, not a student summarizing a textbook.

**Grounded.** Do not dramatize. Do not use retrograde as a source of alarm. Do not write as if planets are agents of punishment.

**Precise.** Every statement earns its place. Remove any sentence that does not add information the reader didn't have before.

**Calm.** The reader may be anxious about a retrograde planet in their chart. The tone must be steady and clarifying, not amplifying.

**Bilingual parity.** Hindi text must be substantive equivalents — not word-for-word translations. A Hindi speaker reading the Hindi version must receive the same quality of insight as an English speaker. Never reduce Hindi answers to summary-level paraphrasing of the English.

## 5a. Information Priority Rule

### The mandatory editorial flow

Every page in this cluster must deliver information in the following sequence. This order is not a preference — it is a production requirement.

```
Science
  ↓
Astronomical Explanation
  ↓
Vedic Interpretation
  ↓
Practical Effects
  ↓
Remedies
  ↓
FAQ
  ↓
CTA
```

### Rule

Writers must never open with an astrological claim before establishing the astronomical phenomenon. A page that begins "Mercury retrograde disrupts communication" before explaining what retrograde motion is — has violated this rule. The skeptical reader leaves immediately. The credulous reader receives a belief without a basis.

### Why this sequence is mandatory

**Science first** earns trust from readers who are not yet convinced that astrology is meaningful. Establishing the physical reality of apparent retrograde motion before making any interpretive claim is the single most effective trust-building move available on these pages.

**Astronomy before astrology** grounds every subsequent interpretation. A reader who understands why a planet appears to move backward is prepared to engage with what that appearance means in the Vedic framework. A reader who has not been given the astronomical basis is being asked to accept interpretation on faith.

**Practical effects only after interpretation** ensures the reader understands the mechanism before receiving the conclusion. Stating "Saturn retrograde delays career advancement" without first establishing what Saturn retrograde is and how Vedic tradition reads it — produces content the reader cannot evaluate or trust.

**Remedies after effects** follows the natural reader journey. The reader must understand the problem before they can consider the solution. Remedies placed before effects read as prescriptions for conditions that have not yet been explained.

**FAQ before CTA** captures residual questions and long-tail intent before asking the reader to act. FAQ is a trust-completion mechanism, not an appendix.

**CTA last** means the reader has received full value before being asked to take an action. A CTA placed before value delivery reads as a sales prompt. A CTA placed after it reads as a natural next step.

### Page-level enforcement

This hierarchy applies to both the hub and all five detail pages. On the hub, the Science and Astronomical Explanation sections establish the cluster-wide foundation. On detail pages, each layer is planet-specific. The sequence cannot be inverted, abbreviated at the top, or reordered for stylistic reasons.

## 6. What Must Be Explained Scientifically

Every page in this cluster must somewhere contain an accurate, accessible explanation of the following. The hub page contains the full explanation. Detail pages include a condensed version specific to that planet.

### Apparent retrograde motion

Retrograde motion is apparent, not real. Planets do not reverse direction. The appearance of backward motion results from the relative speeds of Earth and the planet moving along their respective orbits. This must be stated plainly and early.

### Inner planet vs outer planet mechanics

**Inner planets (Mercury, Venus):** Apparent retrograde occurs as Mercury or Venus overtakes Earth or is overtaken during inferior conjunction. Their retrograde periods are more frequent and shorter.

**Outer planets (Mars, Jupiter, Saturn):** Apparent retrograde occurs near opposition, when Earth — moving faster on its inner orbit — passes the slower outer planet. Their retrograde periods are less frequent and longer.

Writers must not describe both mechanisms identically. Inner and outer planet retrograde arise from different geometric relationships.

### Frequency and duration

Every detail page must state the average frequency and average duration of retrograde for that specific planet. These are not interchangeable across planets and must be researched per planet.

### Direct vs retrograde

Explain what "direct" means (normal apparent forward motion), what "stationary" means (the brief period before and after retrograde when the planet appears motionless), and what "retrograde" means. Do not skip the stationary phase — it is often considered the most intense period by Vedic practitioners.

## 7. What Must Be Explained Astrologically

### Vakri graha

The Sanskrit term for a retrograde planet is *vakri* (वक्री), meaning "crooked" or "curved." Vedic astrology does not universally treat vakri planets as weakened. The classical position is more nuanced: a vakri planet has increased visibility in the night sky (for outer planets near opposition) and is often described as having intensified or concentrated significations. This must be clearly explained. Do not default to the Western interpretation of retrograde as weakening.

### Natal vakri vs transit vakri

A planet retrograde at birth (natal vakri) indicates a person who has an atypical, often inward or unconventional relationship with that planet's themes. A planet retrograde in transit (transit vakri) creates a period of review, revision, or delay in that planet's domain. These are not the same phenomenon. Both must be explained on every detail page.

### The strength question

There is genuine disagreement in Vedic tradition about whether vakri planets are stronger or weaker. Some classical texts treat retrograde as a form of strength; others see it as a distortion. Writers must not pick one position and present it as settled fact. Present the debate, state the most commonly held practical view, and allow the reader to situate their own interpretation.

### What changes during retrograde transit

The planet's themes do not disappear. They do not become harmful by definition. What changes is their mode of operation: matters tend toward review rather than initiation, internalization rather than outward expression. Writers must describe this as a qualitative shift, not a negative judgment.

### Common misconceptions

Every page must address at least three of the following misconceptions:
- Retrograde means the planet is moving backward (it does not)
- Retrograde is always negative (it is not)
- Sun and Moon go retrograde (they do not)
- Rahu and Ketu go retrograde sometimes (they do not — they are always in apparent retrograde)
- Retrograde cancels the planet's positive effects (this is not a settled classical position)
- Western retrograde interpretations apply equally in Vedic astrology (they do not)

The hub links to all five detail pages via planet cards and via the Related Guides section at the bottom. Both links must exist. Do not link to a detail page that is not yet published.

Every detail page ends with "← Retrograde Planets" as a terminal navigation link. This link is mandatory and must not be removed.

The "Other Retrograde Planets" section links to all four sibling detail pages. Each link includes a one-line description distinguishing that planet's retrograde from the current page's planet. Do not link to the current page's own detail page within this section.

### Detail → transit pages
Every detail page links to the corresponding transit page (e.g., `/mercury-transit`) via a Quick Link pill. Transit pages cover timing and predictions. Retrograde detail pages cover meaning and effects. These are complementary, not redundant.

### Detail → planet-in-house pages
Link to the planet-in-house hub (`/planet-in-house`) in the Related Guides or Quick Links section. Do not link to individual planet-in-house pages from the retrograde cluster — that creates maintenance burden.

### Reciprocal linking
Each new retrograde detail page should be linked from the corresponding transit page. This is a content coordination requirement, not a technical one. It requires editorial coordination when transit pages are updated.

### No broken links
A link to a page that does not exist is a production defect. If a page is not yet published, no link is added. The link is added when the page ships.

## 13. FAQ Rules

### Quantity

Hub page: minimum eight questions.  
Detail pages: minimum ten questions.

### Answer length

Minimum four sentences per answer. Maximum eight sentences. Answers that are one to two sentences are rejected. An FAQ answer that cannot be written in four to eight substantive sentences suggests the wrong question was asked.

### Question requirements

**Hub FAQ questions** must be general retrograde questions a first-time reader would ask. At least one question must address the "is retrograde harmful" misconception. At least one must explain Sun/Moon not going retrograde.

### What FAQ questions must not be

- Questions the article already fully answers in the body text (avoid pure repetition)
- Questions that require date-specific answers ("When is the next Mercury retrograde?")

- Leading questions that imply a foregone conclusion ("Why is Mercury retrograde so dangerous?")

### Hindi FAQ answers

Every FAQ answer requires a full Hindi equivalent (`a_hi`). The Hindi answer must be a substantive equivalent, not a one-sentence summary of the English answer. Hindi speakers asking the question deserve the same answer depth.

## 15. Writing Rules

### The 5-sentence test

Read any paragraph aloud. If it could appear unchanged on a competitor's website, rewrite it. Every paragraph must contain at least one observation that is specific to this planet, this cluster, and the Jyotishasha editorial voice.

### The "so what" rule

After every factual claim, the writer must internally ask "so what does this mean for the reader?" If the answer is obvious, it may be implied. If it is not obvious, it must be stated. Facts without consequence are filler.

### Mechanisms, not conclusions

Do not state an outcome without explaining the mechanism. "Mercury retrograde affects communication" is a conclusion. "Mercury retrograde corresponds to a period when communication tends toward review and retrospection rather than new initiation — resulting in misunderstandings that arise from revisiting incomplete agreements rather than launching new ones" is a mechanism. The mechanism is always preferable.

### Specificity over coverage

A detailed treatment of three effects is better than a cursory mention of ten effects. Never pad a section to hit a word count. Every sentence must carry information.

### Naming conventions

| Term | Use this | Not this |
|---|---|---|
| Planet in backward apparent motion | retrograde | "in reverse," "going backward" |
| Sanskrit term | vakri (वक्री) | vakra, vakri graha (use on first mention only) |
| The person with this placement | the native | "you," "they" (be consistent per section) |
| Transit retrograde period | during [Planet] retrograde | "retrograde season" |
| Return to forward motion | direct or direct station | "out of retrograde" |

## 16. What Writers Must Never Do

**Never state that retrograde is universally negative.** It is not. The Vedic classical tradition is not settled on this. State the complexity. Do not flatten it.

**Never import Western retrograde doctrine without attribution.** "Avoid signing contracts during Mercury retrograde" is a Western popular astrology rule. If mentioned, it must be identified as Western popular interpretation and distinguished from Vedic analysis.

**Never claim Sun or Moon go retrograde.** They do not. If a reader asks, explain why.

**Never claim Rahu or Ketu are sometimes direct.** They are always in apparent retrograde. No exceptions.

**Never write fear-based content.** Do not describe retrograde as a time when things will "go wrong," "fall apart," or "be disrupted." These phrases activate anxiety without providing actionable understanding.

**Never write empty comfort content.** "Retrograde is just a time for reflection and inner work" is the opposite failure — so positive as to be useless. Acknowledge real difficulties without dramatizing them.

**Never use these phrases:**
- "It is important to understand..."
- "In Vedic astrology,..." (as an opener — this cluster IS a Vedic astrology cluster; do not state the obvious)
- "powerful placement" / "powerful period"
- "In conclusion..."
- "To summarize..."
- "retrograde season"
- "Mercury is in retrograde" (it should be "Mercury is retrograde" — the "in" is informal Western usage)

**Never write generic retrograde content on a planet-specific page.** If the paragraph applies equally to Mercury retrograde and Jupiter retrograde, it belongs on the hub, not on a detail page.

**Never conflate natal and transit retrograde without labeling the distinction.** Every claim about retrograde effects must be labeled: natal, transit, or both.

**Never write one-sentence FAQ answers.** Minimum four sentences. No exceptions.

## 17. Repetition Rules

### Across the cluster

The hub explains the general phenomenon. Detail pages do not repeat the full hub explanation. Detail pages reference the hub concept and apply it specifically to their planet. A reader should not find the same paragraphs on the hub and a detail page.

### Within a detail page

Each section covers its domain exclusively. The Career section does not mention relationship effects. The Relationship section does not mention health effects. If an effect spans domains (e.g., communication affecting both career and relationships), mention it in the more dominant domain and cross-reference the other with a single sentence.

### Between natal and transit

Do not write a section that conflates natal and transit effects. If a section covers both, use explicit subheadings or clearly labeled paragraphs: "Natal retrograde:" / "Transit retrograde:" as lead-ins. Never mix the two without a signal to the reader.

### Across planets

No planet's detail page may recycle paragraphs from another planet's detail page. Mercury's career section is about Mercury's communication domains. Saturn's career section is about Saturn's authority and delay themes. Structural similarity is expected. Identical sentences are not acceptable.

### The hub planet card and the detail page intro

The card summary (two lines maximum) must not be identical to the detail page opening. The card draws the reader in. The detail page delivers the full treatment.

## 19. AI Writing Rules

This section is a permanent contract governing AI-assisted content generation for every page in the Retrograde Planets cluster and all future sub-clusters. These rules apply to every AI writing session, without exception.

### Cross-planet uniqueness

**Never duplicate paragraphs across planets.** If a paragraph from Mercury's page could appear on Mars's page without change, it is generic and must be rewritten to be planet-specific before delivery.

**Every planet must have unique astronomy.** Mercury and Venus are inner planets with inferior-conjunction retrograde mechanics. Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn are outer planets with opposition-based mechanics. These are physically different phenomena. Do not adapt one planet's astronomy section for another.

**Every planet must have unique Vedic interpretation.** Saturn vakri carries classical implications that differ from Venus vakri. Jupiter vakri in classical texts is not equivalent to Mars vakri. Significations, classical citations, and traditional analysis must be sourced per planet, not transferred between them.

**Every planet must have unique practical effects.** Mercury retrograde does not produce the same life effects as Jupiter retrograde. Career, relationship, and health correspondences differ by planet. Do not reuse effect paragraphs across planets, even as structural templates with substituted planet names.

### Natal vs transit discipline

**Always distinguish Natal Retrograde from Transit Retrograde whenever both are applicable.** This distinction must be labeled explicitly — in the Vedic Meaning section, in every Effects section, and in the Do's and Don'ts sections. Never merge natal and transit effects into a single undifferentiated statement.

### Source discipline

**Never mix Western Astrology into Vedic content.** Western popular retrograde doctrine — avoid contracts, back up your data, expect miscommunication — may appear for contextual contrast only. It must be explicitly labeled as Western popular interpretation and immediately distinguished from the Vedic view. All default content is Vedic.

**Never generate current-cycle dates on evergreen pages.** If a year, month, or specific date appears in generated output for a retrograde detail page or the hub, remove it before delivery. Dates belong on transit pages.

**Verify planet-specific facts before writing.** Retrograde frequency and average duration differ across planets and must not be estimated or borrowed from another planet's data. If the figure is uncertain, flag it for editorial verification rather than guessing.

### Consistency rules

**Maintain factual consistency across the cluster.** If Mercury retrograde occurs 3–4 times per year on the detail page, the hub must reflect the same figure. If a classical text is cited on a detail page, the citation must be accurate and consistent with any other reference to that text in the cluster.

**If generating Do's and Don'ts, verify each item applies specifically to this planet's significations.** A Do or Don't that could appear under any planet's retrograde is rejected. Each item must be traceable to this planet's domains in Vedic tradition.

**If generating Remedies, verify each remedy is associated with this specific planet in Vedic tradition.** Do not transfer remedy prescriptions from one planet to another. Mantras, gemstones, colors, days, and deity associations are planet-specific and must not be mixed.

### Quality rules

**Never write FAQ answers under 4 sentences.** The four-sentence minimum is a hard floor. A one- or two-sentence FAQ answer is returned for expansion before it enters the document.

**Avoid unnecessary repetition within a page.** Each section covers its domain. If a point was made in Overview, it is not restated in Career. If a mechanism was explained in Astronomy, it is referenced — not re-explained — in Vedic Meaning.

**Prefer concise, authoritative language.** A shorter, accurate sentence outperforms a longer, hedged one. Do not add qualifiers to avoid commitment. State what is known. State what is debated as debate. Do not manufacture false certainty, and do not hedge where the classical tradition is clear.

**Run the forbidden phrases check (§16) on all generated output before delivery.** This includes both the English and Hindi versions of every section.

### Hindi rules

**Never reduce Hindi answers to summaries of English answers.** The Hindi version of every section and FAQ answer must be a substantive equivalent. A Hindi speaker reading the Hindi page must receive the same depth of insight as an English speaker reading the English page.

**Do not translate literally.** Hindi content is written for Hindi-speaking searchers with their own search behavior and conceptual vocabulary. Transliterated Sanskrit terms (vakri, graha) are used alongside Devanagari forms. Translate ideas, not sentences.
